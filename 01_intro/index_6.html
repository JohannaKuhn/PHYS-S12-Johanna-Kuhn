I found that when covered with your hands it detects an average of 97.5 of light, but when I increased the amount of light on it it reached to a maximum of 392. I found that I could control this number by adding and subtracting the amount of light on the phototransistor. <center><img src="phototransistor graph.PNG" width="705" ></center> ______________________________________________________________________________________ # Capacitive Sensor I made a rotating capacitive sensor because I wanted to see how the data would change as one side rotates. I included a Servo motor to make one side move 180 degrees and back, so that I could calculate the distance between the two more accurately. First I started by making two circles in Fusion360. Their diameter is 110 mm and I hand cut triangles off the edges so that the alligator clips could attach to the metal. After that I hot glues the metal to the circles and ho glued them opposite of each other on a box. I connected the Servo motor to one side and programmed it to spin 180 degrees. I measured the capacity while it spun. # Here's what the curcuit looks like: <center><img src="6 look.jpg" width="405" ></center> # Here is it in motion: <center><video width="620" controls> <source src="capacity vid.mp4" type="video/mp4"> </video></center> # A graph of my findings with it: <center><img src="capacity graph.PNG" width="705" ></center> The farther the two metals were apart the less capacity was sensed. # Here is the Arduino codes for both: <p> Here is a <a href="phototransister link.html">LINK</a> to the phototransister code. </p> <p> Here is a <a href="capacitive sensor.html">LINK</a> to the Capacitive Sensor code. </p>
